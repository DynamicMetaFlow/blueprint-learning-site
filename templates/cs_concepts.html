CS Concepts and Practices

Introduction

Computer science is the exploration and application of computing practices and concepts. Some of the most common applications of computer science (CS) is to program computers to do tasks including finding information, enabling communication, or increasing productivity. However computers are increasingly  programmed in a wide range of fields to create visual art and music, build scientific simulations, or analyze and even generate literature.

In the sections below we define the CS Practices and Concepts commonly applied across the diversity of computing applications. We introduce CS vocabulary and try to demystify concepts by presenting parallel examples of the other subjects. We also set the bar for a Meaningful CS Unit that schools should aim to provide to all of their students.

Practices

The CS practices below are methods to explore computer science. Each practice is defined  will require time for students to apply concepts, synthesize information from multiple sources, and design and create artifacts.

  • Analyze the impacts of computing
  • Prototype projects connected to their interests
  • Communicate understanding of computing concepts

Each practice description provides background information and an ideal stepwise process. Educators must scaffold those processes to fit the needs of their students. Jump to Practices .

Concepts

Computer science concepts provide a framework for what a computer can and cannot do. There are five top level concepts, each with multiple subconcepts: 1) Abstraction, 2) Algorithms, 3) Programming, 4) Data, 5) Networks.

Each concept and its subconcepts are defined below with demystifying examples of those concepts in Math and English Language Arts (ELA). Jump to Concepts.

Meaningful CS Unit

The goal of Computer Science for All is to provide every student in NYC with a meaningful unit of computer science instruction at every grade band (K-2, 3-5, 6-8, 9-12) by 2025. Meaningful units of CS engage students in:

  • a computer science topic of exploration aligned to a school’s culture and/or vision,
  • all three CS practices appropriately levelled by grade,
  • a subset of CS concepts that always includes programming appropriately levelled by grade,
  • and a performance task that assesses students on their mastery of concepts & practices.

Practices (P)

Analyze the impacts of computing (AN)

Government, industry, and large organizations use the principles of computing to program computers to handle banking, surveillance, communication, and manufacturing. In between,  a diversity of groups use computing and computers to:

- accomplish complex tasks like driving cars, playing Jeopardy, or sequencing genes

- express themselves through graphics, games, or creative computing

- steal money, property, or information

- automate small or mundane tasks

- make decisions like forecasting the weather, recidivism, or amount of traffic

These are just a few examples. The breadth and depth of computing are growing fast. People of all backgrounds must evaluate and respond to the ways that computing is affecting their lives, their families and friends, and the systems in which they live . In order to analyze the social impacts of computing, students should:

  1. Describe an application of computing by detailing who, what, where, when. Try to describe the application with data and by highlighting the relevant computing concepts
  2. Analyze the description and the data for patterns, general characteristics, or anomalies. Ask specific questions related to computing concepts
  3. Interpret the trends or insights from your analysis by looking for evidence to support an explanation. Test the evidence by researching similar cases, running experiments, or creating models.
  4. Evaluate the application of computing based on your investigation. What are the benefits and harms, to who, how much, when and how? What are potential modifications and the arguments for and against?

(Describe, Analyze, Interpret, Evaluate from Lauren McCarthy at Learning to Teach, Teaching to Learn by SFPC 15 Jan 2017)

Prototype projects connected to student interests (PR)

Computer science concepts are best explored and understood through hands-on experience building projects that are connected to students’ interests. CS concepts are tangible when applied using the "Engineering Design Process": Ask, Research, Imagine, Plan, Create, Test, Improve (see reference below). The final step in the EDP is Improve. In order to improve a prototype it is very important for students to ask for strong formative feedback from peers or teammates and to maintain a growth mindset so they can repeat the steps of the EDP ( iteration). The process should be repeated as much as time allows because not only does it help students develop their prototypes, but it also helps students build a mental model of computing concepts.

In computer science, an important mental model to build is the notional machine. "A robust mental model of a notional machine means that you can talk about what a computer can do, completely apart from what a computer is doing in any particular role or function." ( Guzdial , 24 May 2012).

Even famous computer scientists like Ada Lovelace, Grace Hopper , and Margaret Hamilton  followed a process like the EDP in order to build up to an understanding of what a computer can do.

To practice the Engineering Design Process, students should ideally work in small groups to iteratively build prototypes using the following steps:

  1. Ask: identify the need & constraints
  2. Research: the problem using trusted sources, interviews, etc.
  3. Imagine: develop possible solutions using ideation techniques
  4. Plan: select a promising solution and identify materials, timeline, and group roles
  5. Create: build a prototype to test solution using paper, digital media, or code
  6. Test: evaluate prototype through user testing or formative feedback
  7. Improve: repeat process using feedback to identify new needs and constraints

Communicate understanding of computing concepts (CO)

Students are expected to explain their thinking in writing or orally in computer science as they are in all subjects. They must communicate their analysis of computing impacts and how they applied computing concepts while prototyping projects. In addition to written and verbal communication, CS gives students a chance to express themselves through their prototypes. For example, a student that wants to display:

  • her understanding of algorithms can write her program to explain to the user each step in her algorithm as it is happening.
  • his mastery of the programming syntax for IF statements, can write a program that implements a complex, nested IF statement with descriptive comments  in the program
  • her arguments around the impacts of basing journalism on clicks, likes and shares can build an interactive program that fact checks sample articles that users like

To communicate understanding of computing concepts, students should:

  1. Choose a computing concept: Focus on concepts central to the students’ work, concepts the student understands deeply through application.
  2. Describe the audience: Consider and plan on presenting ideas to broader audiences - the entire class, all teachers in the school, the community, etc. Empathize and think critically about the backgrounds, knowledge, and values of the audience.
  3. Select a medium: Select media that will reach the intended audience, interactive programs, video, audio, slide decks, blog posts, papers, or instructional activities.
  4. Connect concept to analysis or prototype: Explicitly show and explain the application of a computing concept by showing code from a prototype and explaining its significance in the program or by creating a relevant example that explains the concept and how it can be used.
  5. Present thoughts, ideas, or arguments: Present or publish the work and receive formative feedback in the form of question and answer, comments, or user testing. Publishing work and capturing feedback as part of digital portfolios helps work persist after the unit or class ends.

Concepts (C)

Abstraction (AB)

An abstraction represents a simplified idea or problem derived by ignoring details and using patterns or general characteristics. A simple example of a CS abstraction is a programming language, they allow programmers to give complex instructions to computers without worrying about the bits & bytes!

Decomposition (D)

Ideas, problems, or projects are broken down into component parts to set the stage for deeper analysis.

145 can be decomposed as 100 + 40 + 5.

A sentence can be decomposed into a subject and predicate.

Pattern Recognition (PR)

Decomposed component parts are examined to find patterns like similarities, repetition, conditional relationships, or nested relationships.

2, 4, 6, 8, 10 are even numbers,  1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11 are odd numbers

A poem can have rhyme schemes or meter.

Generalization & Detail Removal (GDR)

Component parts are grouped by general characteristics and filter out unnecessary details.

Rounding or estimating removes details to provide a general understanding.

Summarizing a novel requires removing details and writing generally.

Modularity (M)

A process that completes a single task is more useful when it can be chained together with other processes to accomplish something more complex.

Interfaces (I)

Interfaces help users  complete a tasks while hiding details of the overall process.

Function machines in math that take inputs and produce outputs hide the math being done.

Words have origins, usages, and histories that are hidden away, but writers and speakers can still use them effectively.

Algorithms (AL)

Algorithms are generalized and repeatable sequence of instructions that achieve a particular purpose and output, given a set of inputs. It's important to understand when, why, and how to implement an algorithm and to consider who or what might be affected.

Application (A)

Understanding where, when, why and how to apply algorithms generally, and which algorithm to apply in a given context.

Decomposing a word problem to understand when we should add, subtract, multiply, or divide.

Stories, dramas, and poems have different structures - chapters, scenes, stanzas - that should be applied in different situations.

Programming (P)

Programming is about giving instructions to computers. Programming can be done through a growing variety of languages. Each language is translated from human-readable code into machine code that can be completed  by the computer’s central processing unit, CPU.

Languages (L)

Programming languages have different applications and require different amounts of prior knowledge. Some languages can only be run on specific hardware while others run with software like a web browser.

Depending on the mathematical problem, simple arithmetic, ratios and fractions, geometry, or algebra will need to be applied.

Within English there are different writing or speaking styles that are used depending on the situation - research papers, persuasive essays, letters, etc.

Syntax (S)

All programming languages have a set of commands, or reserved words, and grammar rules that must be followed.

Mathematical notation for simple addition problems to complex algebraic formulas follows a specific syntax.

Grammar rules are the syntax of the English language.

Development Environments (DE)

Programmers use development environments to create applications - these environments have an editor to write code, run and show a program’s output, and log errors that arise.

Calculators can be used to quickly show the results of a calculation.

Word processors or other digital media do show our errors in the form of spell and grammar checking.

Collaboration (C)

Collaborating on code is complex because each line is part of a larger algorithm or abstraction. Clearly setting team roles, saving versions along the way, and using parallel versions are some ways to manage the complexity.

A large composition can be completed by multiple people by assigned different sections for each to write or assigning roles such as writer and proofreader.

Data (D)

Computers can be used to collect, store, and analyze massive amounts of data quickly and reliably. Computers programs can use data to make decisions or automate tasks.

Sensors & Sources (SS)

Data is collected by sensors such as video cameras or thermostats or from other sources such as government data or your digital activity. Data from sensors and sources must always be cleaned .

Data can be collected from the class such as heights, interests, country of origin, etc.

If students are taking measurements of physical spaces, rulers can be seen as analogous to sensors.

Data Abstraction & Storage (DAS)

Data is represented in computers as binary but humans save and use data on computer as lists, databases, key-value pairs, etc.

Tables of rows and columns are an example of data representation.

Plot outlines or diagrams represent complex data in a structure form. This is a powerful because it shows data can be lost when represented.

Transformation & Visualization (TV)

Computers make looking at data easier by allowing us to quickly visualize data in different ways and to “break up a big problem into manageable pieces, operate on each piece independently and then put all the pieces back together.”

When (x,y) points are plotted, it becomes easier to see their relationship.

If the (x,y) points are very far apart, the scale of a graph must be adjusted.

Feedback Loops & Automation (FLA)

Automation is controlled by data. Some automation uses data from outside the automated system, like time. Other automation uses data on the output of the system, like temperature.

Proofreading provides closed-loop feedback (feedback on the output).

Networks (N)

Networks, like the Internet, allow computers to interface with other computers through a set of rules, or protocols, that define how computers send and receive data. Protocols and standards are created and agreed upon by groups of people.

Trust (T)

The common thread behind issues of security, privacy and consent is trust. Whenever we connect to a network, we must establish a level of trust that aligns with our security and privacy needs and implement and monitor protocolsto protect those needs.

When doing research or citing sources it is important to establish a level of trust in order to critically approach source materials.

Protocols (PRO)

In order to send data from one device to another, they must be synchronized, share a syntax for the data, share a method of encryption and have a way to report and recover from errors.

Protocols for a math assignment include requirements like showing work that make communication between student and teacher easier.

Protocols for a writing assignment include the content that must be included, the length of the paper, and how it should be submitted.

Physical Internet (PI)

It is critical to understand the infrastructure of the Internet including the hardware, companies, governing bodies, etc. that connect your computer to internet exchanges where massive networks cross connect to the undersea fiber optic cables that connect the world (not satellites). 

Markup (M)

Hypertext Markup Language (HTML) is the standard way to publish information or applications on the Internet. It is a standard agreed upon by the Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF). It is not a programming language as it doesn’t use logic.

Mathematics has standard representations of different expressions: ½ is a fraction, 1 x 2 is multiplication, etc.

Underlining titles, indenting paragraphs, starting sentences with capitalization and ending them with punctuation are all examples of publishing standards in English.
