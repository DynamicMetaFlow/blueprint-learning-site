Introduction

Computer science is the exploration and application of computing practices and concepts. Some of the most common applications of computer science (CS) is to program computers to do tasks including finding information, enabling communication, or increasing productivity. However computers are increasingly  programmed in a wide range of fields to create visual art and music, build scientific simulations, or analyze and even generate literature.

 the impacts of computing (AN)

Government, industry, and large organizations use the principles of computing to program computers to handle banking, surveillance, communication, and manufacturing. In between,  a diversity of groups use computing and computers to:

- accomplish complex tasks like driving cars, playing Jeopardy, or sequencing genes

- express themselves through graphics, games, or creative computing

- steal money, property, or information

- automate small or mundane tasks

- make decisions like forecasting the weather, recidivism, or amount of traffic

These are just a few examples. The breadth and depth of computing are growing fast. People of all backgrounds must evaluate and respond to the ways that computing is affecting their lives, their families and friends, and the systems in which they live . In order to analyze the social impacts of computing, students should:

 understanding of computing concepts (CO)

Students are expected to explain their thinking in writing or orally in computer science as they are in all subjects. They must communicate their analysis of computing impacts and how they applied computing concepts while prototyping projects. In addition to written and verbal communication, CS gives students a chance to express themselves through their prototypes. For example, a student that wants to display:

  • her understanding of algorithms can write her program to explain to the user each step in her algorithm as it is happening.
  • comments  in the program
  •  program that fact checks sample articles that users like

To communicate understanding of computing concepts, students should:

  1.  Focus on concepts central to the students’ work, concepts the student understands deeply through application.
  2. Consider and plan on presenting ideas to broader audiences - the entire class, all teachers in the school, the community, etc. Empathize and think critically about the backgrounds, knowledge, and values of the audience.
  3. Select media that will reach the intended audience, interactive programs, video, audio, slide decks, blog posts, papers, or instructional activities.
  4. Explicitly show and explain the application of a computing concept by showing code from a prototype and explaining its significance in the program or by creating a relevant example that explains the concept and how it can be used.
  5. Present or publish the work and receive formative feedback in the form of question and answer, comments, or user testing. Publishing work and capturing feedback as part of digital portfolios helps work persist after the unit or class ends.

Decomposition (D)

Ideas, problems, or projects are broken down into component parts to set the stage for deeper analysis.

145 can be decomposed as 100 + 40 + 5.

Pattern Recognition (PR)

Decomposed component parts are examined to find patterns like similarities, repetition, conditional relationships, or nested relationships.

2, 4, 6, 8, 10 are even numbers,  1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11 are odd numbers

Component parts are grouped by general characteristics and filter out unnecessary details.

Rounding or estimating removes details to provide a general understanding.

Summarizing a novel requires removing details and writing generally.

A process that completes a single task is more useful when it can be chained together with other processes to accomplish something more complex.
